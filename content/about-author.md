I was born an ‘observer’. As far back as I can remember, I was ‘pulled’ by every baby within crying or cooing range. I loved everything about babies. Their facial expressions and every sound they uttered mesmerized me. I just knew they were perfect and full of ‘themselves’. Their simple trust in people was inherent in their unwavering gazes into the eyes of any admirer.  I often dreamed of ‘getting’ a baby brother, but I was destined to remain the youngest in my family.  Observing babies was the only way for me to bring them into my life. I remember following babies as they were carted down grocery store aisles, living in my friend’s homes, or being pushed along the sidewalks as they snuggled inside baby carriages.

  


As a child I would remember my mom singing full throttle on weekend mornings while washing dishes. She had a really sweet soprano voice that would lovingly fill our small cape cod style house in Detroit, Michigan. I thought she probably could have been a professional singer if she wanted. She was a happy person, even though life dealt her a hard hand of being deserted by her husband who was one of many WWII veterans who deserted their families after returning from war.   

  


So my mom was left to raise her three children aged 13, 10, and 6 years old without their father’s presence or financial support. To support us, she asked our maternal grandmother to move into our home so she could take a full time union job in a potato chip factory where her sister’s husband was a manager. I could not help but notice how much my mom enjoyed frying chips on a hot conveyor line. She delighted in explaining to anyone who would take any interest in learning about the dynamics of correctly frying the perfect potato chip. She would firmly inform them that browned chips are not burned, they were just chips with a higher sugar count! She put herself into that work with heart and soul! Maybe because she was grateful for it as it kept food on the table and a roof over our heads! I cannot remember a time when mom yelled or lost patience with me throughout my childhood. I observed and reasoned that feeling good about your work seemed to offer something for everyone, not just for the person working.

  


My father’s influence on my desire to help others pull into their lives the people, places, and things that would make them happy, was another matter entirely. He left home when he was forty years old, shortly after I turned six years old.  No one knew why or where, he just left one hot August day without a trace in the family’s new gray and white Chevy sedan. Through my successful Social Security search many decades later, I discovered that he recently died of natural causes at the age of eighty one in Albuquerque, New Mexico.  I sometimes wonder if he ever ‘checked in on us’ while us kids were growing up, getting married and raising our own children.

 

I learned later from my aunt, that my dad, her brother, loved sports and if any kind of ball was being thrown anywhere in his proximity, he was even happier. He attended a Catholic seminary high school to prepare for priesthood. My grandmother was a fervently religious woman and felt blessed that her son was in training to become a Catholic priest.  However, as fate would have it, one night, a priest happened to check my dad’s room and found that the ‘lump’ in his bed was actually pillows lined up neatly in a row. Later that night, as my dad crept quietly back into his dorm room, he was greeted heartily by the priest patiently sitting on his ‘pillow’ bed.  It turned out that my dad just could not resist joining in an evening game of touch football down the street with the public high school kids. After a long conversation, the priest and my dad came to the joint conclusion that maybe my dad just wasn't ‘priestly material’ afterall.

  


He left that school and upon his return home, he began to help his mother with her catering business. Years later, he met my mom at a church function that his mom was catering.  The moment he saw my mom, he told his buddies that she was the woman he was going to marry! He invited himself to her table and sat next to her to meet her. Afterwards, when my mom heard what he was saying about her, she thought it incredulous that anyone could know that at first sight. Her sister later shared with me that after meeting my dad, she could tell my mom was interested in him more than any other man she had dated before. My dad persisted, pursued, and won her heart. They soon married and started a family.  First came my brother, three years later my sister, and in four more years, I was born.

  


My brother never shared our father’s intense interest in sports as a kid. It became increasingly harder through the years for my brother to be anywhere near our dad. Our dad grew less patient with him, taking his disappointments out in ways that were emotionally and physically hurtful to my brother. As I tried to piece his life back together, everything started to unravel for him involving jobs, marriage, and family life.  Add to this the fact that our dad could not stay interested in his string of non-sport related jobs. I wonder if he had been able to actually connect with his own strong interest in sports, he might not have tried to live vicariously through his son.  He could have fully embraced his own life.  He could have been an entirely different husband and father if he had connected with his own innate interests.

  


My dad’s younger sister was equally surprised by the drastic changes in her brother’s temperament as an adult. My aunt told me he was always so very thoughtful, caring, and kind to her while they were growing up. He was the best brother a girl could have! She wondered what happened in his life that he could desert his young family. I get a strong sense of the importance of honoring our interests so that we bring our best Selves and best Gifts to share with our family and community.  It is important.  It simply is.  So my work was also influenced by my mom and dad’s life choices.

  


But I believe it was my aunt, my dad’s sister, who may have had the earliest, most powerful influence on my life’s direction in wanting to see people living happy lives that they love.

I attended a Catholic elementary school for eight years. I observed that some of the Dominican nuns were unhappy and frustrated with their teaching jobs. Why else would girls be marched out of their classroom to the hallway drinking fountain and have their heads placed under the path of the cold water which would instantly transform their teased hair (popular at the time) into dripping wet tresses. And, if they wore nail polish, a sudden sharp whack on the back of their hands with a wood ruler would tell them how improper they were. No warnings, no conversations, just kids being frightened by angry unhappy nuns “how to be in their world!’. It really affected me in a big way seeing kids being treated so badly. I felt so sorry for any targeted classmates treated in these unkind ways. But I also felt so sorry about the nuns’ obvious unhappiness with their lives, that I would occasionally bake them heart shaped cakes for their birthdays. (Since I lived directly across the street from my school, I decided I was the best person for that job.) I once gifted two goldfish to a nun thinking they would bring happiness to her in her classroom. (She returned them to me right when school ended for the summer!) The Sisters appeared not to be enjoying teaching kids at all; some I suspected actually hated it. I believed that they would have been kinder in another line of work.

  


I was able to see that a lot of my school's nuns were unhappy, they were not just ‘mean women’. And this conclusion at my young age was because my aunt was a Franciscan nun who was always kind, laughed easily, and was a joy to be around! I personally ‘got it’ that nuns could be happy if they truly liked their life’s work. Throughout her fifty plus years of teaching elementary grade school kids, my aunt was aligned and happy with her line of work. Later in life, she shared with me that she had always wanted to become a nun from an early age.  She chose this line of work because she wanted it!  Her “angels”, as she called her students, were the lucky ones!

  


When I married and started my own family, I stayed home with my two sons while they were young.  I loved being with my children and did not want to hand them over to a ‘stranger’ for care. I felt so strongly about my desire to raise them myself. (Looking back on my love of seeing babies, I think I was born to be a mother.)

  


When it came time to bring home a paycheck at my husband’s request (which was understandable), I struggled with how to connect myself with work that attracted me. I intrinsically knew the importance of connecting with work that would hold meaning for me. I soon read about a new company moving into my city in the newspaper. It looked interesting and I made an appointment to meet the Sales Manager.  He hired me during that meeting because I was genuine in expressing my desire to work in outside sales where I could be creative and help people with solutions to address their communication issues reaching their employees on the job.  I began to sell pagers throughout the county!  I had to find some connection, some ‘life’ in a job.  An outside Sales Representative job gave me the independence and opportunity to be creatively helpful! It fit me.  

  


The work that fit me very well was as a Branch Manager for two profit centers at an international temporary staffing company. My role was managing the branches’ operations and developing new contracts with companies in outside sales presentations. I enjoyed getting contracts with companies to call my branches when they needed temporary employees to work for them. I could not help but notice the burning desire of potential temporary employees to find work that would fit them, work that they could look forward to each day.  I will always remember those faces full of hope and expectation as they were given a new job assignment by my Service Representatives! Many of these temporary placements became permanent hires by major companies in my branch's territory. This opened up many opportunities for the temps that would otherwise not be available for them!

  


I would share two important working guidelines with each Service Representative that I hired.  The first was, “Bring YOURSELF to work each day.” I would teach them customer service skills,  but I would share that to be most effective, they would have to be their authentic selves when working here. Secondly, I would tell them, “If you’re not making mistakes, you’re standing still”. Now, I know this may sound like not a good thing to tell an employee.  But I reasoned that they knew their jobs and because they were on the front line seeing all the results of their work, I wanted them to feel they could be creative with solutions that would favorably impact the lives of their temporary employees and our customer companies. I believe it is important to be in an environment that accepts ‘who you are’. This was my gift to my employees.

  


I completed a Bachelor of Arts in Management degree while working. My heart would have chosen to continue with Psychology, which is what I had originally pursued after high school.  Given my present corporate work, I chose what seemed to be the most logical path to getting a degree. I did not have the courage to listen to my own advice to “bring YOURSELF” to life by following your innate interests.  I was in business, so I pursued a business degree. Looking back, I can now ask myself, “where was I when I needed me”? I might have ventured outside of this area of comfort and studied psychology/counseling.

  


I ended my thirty year marriage so that I could escape the growing disapproval of my husband and earnestly pursue work of helping kids connect with their innate interests. My husband repeatedly wondered how I would make a big income helping kids. I could not answer his 

question to his satisfaction. The more he pushed for money, the more distant we became.  I really wanted to help kids recognize and connect with their innate interests while growing up in whatever big or small ways they could. I felt this was a way for them to grow up happy and even connect them with a path to their happiness in adulthood. He and I both needed a break from this cycle of trying to get the other person to live in a way that did not allow them to live authentically. I discovered that ‘I’ was not asleep afterall.  I left my marriage. It was the best decision.

  


I was naturally drawn to observe other people’s happiness and interests as a very young child.  I firmly believe that following one’s heartfelt interests leads to happiness, for ourselves and every person we meet. I want to make it as easy as possible for others, especially youths to live congruent lives. A crucial first step is to recognize and help them recognize or discover what interests them strongly. The next step is to honor their interests by helping them connect in ways, both big and small, with their chosen interests. Honoring interests is a simple ABC process of Asking, Brainstorming and Connecting them with their interests while the interest remains strong. 

  


I founded youth workshops in after school programs which included helping them create Public Service Announcements, (PSA’s) that aired on Comcast TV . These were students who shared an interest in video and media production. They were delighted to see their work appear on TV!

  


I also spent over a decade working at a non-profit as an Employment Specialist connecting people with developmental disabilities for paid jobs in Lane County in Oregon.  I focused on finding work that would align as closely as possible to their desired interests.  I did not just find job openings for them.  I was creative and became a storyteller with targeted employers.  I told genuine stories of each client to the hiring manager and they became interested in helping my clients connect with work that they were wanting to do.

The message I’m sending out is to ‘allow and help kids maintain their true Selves’. Children cannot always easily honor their interests by themselves. They need the circle of adults in their lives to not squash their dreams. Parents, out of love and concern, may squash their kids' dreams because they fear it would not give them high paying jobs to support themselves in the future. I am collecting and writing short stories of people’s lives, highlighting how they discovered what naturally pulled them and how they managed to bring it into their lives. This honoring of interest's work is in my heart and soul.

  


I am gathering a series of life stories entitled, ‘It’s Monday Morning . . .’ which I am sharing.  It’s a sad statistic – heart attacks in the US happen most often on Mondays in the morning.  Researchers theorize the Monday risk could relate to the stress of the workweek. In my opinion, maybe too many people are heading off to jobs that don’t ‘fit’ their spirit. I will continue to point my nose in the direction of my interest to help youths live authentic lives by finding and honoring their interests and following what excites them.
